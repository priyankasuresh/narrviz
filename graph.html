Earlier it was called Sangeetha Akaara but because the male performers take "sakhibhaav roop", it got renamed to Gotipua. There are four divisions in a traditional composition - mangalacharan, pallavi, abhinay and bhanda nrutya. On our visit to Raghurajpur, we learned about the process of making the famous Pattachitra painting, and the ordeal that goes on behind the scenes for one such work of art. All the pattachitra paintings are basically made of six colors. The villagers will make the color at a Govt restricted area where they get the stones. The artists and villagers source and make their own sustainable paints, gum and canvas. Paintings are generally used after two or three years of making to reduce the poisonous qualities and to increase tenacity. The main color is white and is made of sea shell. Boiling and curing of this shell makes white paint. As it is close to the Indian ocean, shells are easily procured to make white paint and deduce other paints from it. Hengula makes red and is also very expensive at about 15,000/kg. When working with red hengula and haritaal , if you paint immediately after making, then the painting turns black. This is also used to make the painting look antique. Black is made of “baniglam soot”, is the cheapest paint to make.Indigo made of Nilgiri stone. Haritaal makes yellow and is poisonous if you consume it. So it is handled carefully. The basic six colors are then mixed to procure other colors and only these are used in a single painting to maintain uniformity. All colors are mixed with udapal gum for the adhesive quality and binding. The colors would wear off so this helps with fixing also. In Raghurajpur, the artists display their art on the walls. It could be anything from narratives of their family deities to pictures depicting everyday life. The Sun Temple of Konark was constructed in the mid 13th century by Raja Narasinghs Deva-I of the Ganga Dynasty. It is conceived in the form of a huge chariot drawn by seven spirited horses on 12 pairs of wheels--after the mythical seven horse chariot of Sun God.The Sun Temple has lost its many of its original structures to time and disrepair, but the remaining structures confirm till today the infinite imaginative power of the artistes of the time and their inspiring contribution to Vaisnav Culture.The temple follows the traditional style of Kalinga architecture. It is carefully oriented towards the east so that the first rays of sunrise strikes the principal entrance.[4] The temple is built from Khondalite rocks.The original temple had a main sanctum sanctorum (vimana), which was supposedly 229 feet[6] (70 m) tall. Due to the weight of the superstructure (70 m tall) and weak soil of the area, the main vimana fell in 1837.[9] The audience hall (Jagamohana), which is about 128 feet (39 m) tall, still stands and is the principal structure in the surviving ruins. Among the structures, which have survived to the current day, are the dance hall (Nata mandira) and dining hall (Bhoga mandapa).The monument was also called the Black Pagoda'(Kaala Pagoda) by the European sailors. Konark Sun Temple used Iron beams for its structure.The temple complex is in the shape of a gigantic chariot, having elaborately carved stone wheels, pillars and walls.

Patachitra is an ancient folk-art, which has a long history of 2500 years to its development and contribution to our society. Now this kind of folk-art has become a popular folk-art in Indian society and has crossed Indian border to foreign lands. The art of storytelling has been intrinsic to the culture of the region since days of yore and this has manifested itself in diverse art forms, Patachitra being one of them. Pats were scrolls on which mythological or epic stories were painted in a sequence – this would then be carried by Patuas who would travel from one village to another unrolling and showcasing these and singing of various mythological stories. However, the use of pats was not only limited to storytelling – this form of art was a part and parcel of the people’s daily life. Almost every house had its share of patachitra. Patachitra, known for its luminous play of colours, is of two kinds— art on a broad sheet of folded cloth and ‘eye-art’ on a short piece of fabric. The fabric in fact forms the base for pat art. Clay, cow-dung and some sticky elements are skillfully added to the fabric. When dried, the fabric becomes tough but mellow enough to sustain the stroke of the artist’s brush. Pata artists draw on it religious motifs, such as gods and goddesses, Puranic stories, slokas, etc.

Apart from the well-known Odissi dance, Odisha has given numerous dances less known to the outside world. Most of the dance-form are developed from the rituals and practices carried out by over hundred tribes spread all over Odisha and neighbouring states. The contents of which revolves around the god and goddesses worshiped by the people. Well known among them are Chaau Dance, Kanchi Abhijana, and Gotipua Dance. The word Goti Pua literally means Single boy. This dance is performed by young boys who dress up as female. The acrobatic figures practiced to praise Lord Jagannath or Lord Krishna. Boys do not cut their hair and wove flowers while hair-dressing, to look natural like a girl. The face make-up includes Kajal (traditional black liner), Bindi (red dot on forehead) and patterns made on face with sandalwood paste. Traditional dress is called as Kanchula consisting a bright coloured blouse and waist-wrap called Nibibandha. jewellery is made with beads (a common unit for making jewellery among the local tribes). The costume, jewellery and the bells all are considered to be sacred items.
The Jagannath Temple of Puri is an important Hindu temple dedicated to Lord Jagannath, a form of Vishnu, located on the eastern coast of India, at Puri in the state of Odisha. The temple is an important pilgrimage destination and one of the four great 'Char Dham' pilgrimage sites, found at India's four cardinal points. The huge temple complex covers an area of over 400,000 square feet (37,000 m2), and is surrounded by a high fortified wall. This 20 feet (6.1 m) high wall is known as Meghanada Pacheri.[23] Another wall known as kurma bedha surrounds the main temple.[24] It contains at least 120 temples and shrines. With its sculptural richness and fluidity of the Oriya style of temple architecture, it is one of the most magnificent monuments of India. The temple's kitchen is considered as the largest kitchen in the world. All food is cooked following rules as prescribed by Hindu religious texts, the food cooked is pure vegetarian without using onions and garlic, in earthen pots with water drawn from two special wells near the kitchen called Ganga and Yamuna on wood.The main temple is a curvilinear temple and crowning the top is the 'srichakra' (an eight spoked wheel) of Vishnu. Also known as the "Nilachakra", it is made out of Ashtadhatu and is considered sacrosanct.[27] Among the existing temples in Orissa, the temple of Shri Jagannath is the highest. Temple has 4 entrances in all directions. The temple remains open from 5 am to 12 midnight. Unlike many other temples devotees can go behind the idols (go round the idols). All devotees are allowed to go right up to the deities during the Sahana Mela without paying any fees. There is a statue of Aruna the charioteer of the Sun God on top of the Aruna Stambha in front of the Singhadwara.The market streets around the temple is striving with culture, selling colorful knick-knacks. A huge population of Puri depends on the temple for their livelihood. The Singahdwara, which in Sanskrit means The Lion Gate, is one of the four gates to the temple and forms the Main entrance. Jagannath (Odia: ଜଗନ୍ନାଥ, IAST: Jàgannātha, or Jagannatha) literally means "Lord of the Universe" and is a deity worshipped in regional traditions of Hinduism and Buddhism in India and Bangladesh. Jagannath is considered a form of Vishnu.[1] He is a part of a triad along with his brother Balabhadra and sister Subhadra. To some Vaishnava Hindus, Jagannath is an abstract representation of Krishna; to some Shaiva and Shakta Hindus, he is a symmetry-filled tantric representation of Bhairava; to some Buddhists, he is symbolism for Buddha in the Buddha-Sangha-Dhamma triad; to some Jains, his name and his festive rituals are derived from Jeenanath of Jainism tradition. A circumstantial evidence that links Jagannath deity to Buddhism is the Ratha-Yatra festival for Jagannath, the stupa-like shape of the temple and a dharmachakra-like discus (chakra) at the top of the spire.His name does not appear in the traditional Dashavatara (ten avatars) of Vishnu,[5] though in certain Odia literature, Jagannath has been treated as the Ninth avatar.One of the most popular legends associated with Jagannath is that of Kanchi Avijana (or "Conquest of Kanchi"), also termed as "Kanchi-Kaveri". The eyes to show Jagannath is popular in Odishi art and it is known as Patita Pavan.The Jagannath triad are usually worshipped in the sanctum of the temple, but once during the month of Asadha (rainy season of Odisha, usually falling on the month of June or July), they are brought out onto the Bada Danda (Puri's main high street) and travel 3 km to the Shri Gundicha Temple, in huge chariots, allowing the public to have Darshan (i.e., holy view).The icon of Jagannath is a carved and decorated wooden stump with large round eyes and a symmetric face, and the icon has a conspicuous absence of hands or legs. The worship procedures, sacraments and rituals associated with Jagannath are syncretic,[2] and include rites that are uncommon in Hinduism. When shown with Balabhadra and Shubhadra, he is identifiable from his circular eyes compared to oval or almond shape shape of the other two abstract icons. Further, his icon is dark, while Balabhadra's face is white, and Subhadra icon is yellow. The third difference is the flat head of Jagannath icon, compared to semi-circular carved heads of the other two.
